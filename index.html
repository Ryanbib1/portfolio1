Phase 1: Exploring Digital Media


Project Fuego

We start the semester with Project Fuego. This was my second time building a site from a template. It was cool to see again how the code could totally change the vibe of the page.

F1 Webtext Experiment

For this one, I got to bring my interest in F1 into a project. This was all about learning HTML from the ground up and figuring out how to make a site that wasn't just a straight line. Way more to it than I thought!

Multi-Page Challenge

This project was all about building a site with multiple pages and making sure they all linked up correctly. Drawing out the wireframes is nice it helps us know how is everthing works and made it feel like I was building a real website.

Phase 1 Course Goals


1. Foster the creative habits of mind

I've always just done assignments to get them done. But with the F1 Webtext, something just clicked. Since I actually care about F1, I started asking myself, "What would I want to see on a site like this?" It stopped feeling like a school project and more like something I was making for myself or for someone who just knows F1 and wants to know more about it.. It was the first time I felt like I was actually creating something, not just because of grades.

2. Engage with others in a workshop environment.

Showing my work to my group is kinda scary, not gonna lie Josh is surprised that I can build a website like this. When I showed off the Multi-Page Challenge, I thought my navigation was super obvious. But someone else got lost in like two seconds. My first thought was, "No, you're just using it wrong!" But then I realized I was the one who was wrong. What's obvious to me isn't obvious to everyone. It was a humbling reality check that I can't just design for myself.

3. Think deeply about craft.

My idea of craft totally changed with the Multi-Page Challenge. It’s one thing to make a single page look good, but it’s a whole other thing to make an entire site feel connected. I remember getting so frustrated because my navigation bar would be slightly different on each page, or a link would be broken. It felt messy. That’s when I realized craft isn't just about the flashy parts, it’s about consistency and making sure the whole experience feels solid for the user, not like it's about to fall apart.

4. Engage in sustained research and skills development.

I got so stuck on the Multi-Page Challenge. My links were just broken, and it was super frustrating because I had no idea why. I spent what felt like forever on Google, reading stuff that didn't make sense, until I finally found one post on web3 school that explained it. Making a website by studying on your own are actually great(using ai can also help you student if you use as a correct way)The feeling of fixing it myself was awesome. It taught me that being good at this stuff isn't about knowing all the answers, it's about being stubborn enough to find them when you're totally lost.

5. Learn to present your work

Honestly, putting this portfolio together has been its own kind of project. I thought you just put your work on a page and that was it. But when I had to actually write the descriptions for my F1 Webtext and Multi-Page Challenge, I kind of blanked. It's surprisingly hard to explain what you did and why it matters. It forced me to actually think about my process. I'm realizing that if you can't explain what you learned, maybe you haven't fully learned it yet.
